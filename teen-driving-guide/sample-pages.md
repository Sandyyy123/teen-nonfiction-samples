## MODULE 1 — BEFORE YOU EVEN TOUCH THE KEYS

### The Thing Nobody Tells New Drivers

Here's the truth: most new drivers focus entirely on the driving part and skip the 10 minutes before it that would prevent half their problems.

Before you start the car, you should know where you're going, that your mirrors are set right, and that nothing about the car is telling you something's wrong. This takes maybe 90 seconds. Most experienced drivers do it without thinking. You'll have to be intentional about it until it becomes automatic.

---

### The Pre-Drive Checklist (Do This Every Time)

**Outside the car:**
- Walk around the car once — look for anything obviously wrong (flat tire, something blocking the exhaust, something under the car)
- Check that nothing is behind or in front of the car if you're parked in a tight space

**Inside the car:**
- Adjust your seat — you should reach all pedals fully without stretching, and your wrists should rest on top of the steering wheel at 12 o'clock
- Adjust mirrors — you should see the back corners of your own car on the inner edge of each side mirror, and the horizon should sit in the middle of your rearview mirror
- Seatbelt on before you start the engine — not after
- Phone face-down or in the glovebox before you put it in drive

**Dashboard check:**
- Any warning lights? Red means stop and figure it out. Yellow means pay attention. If your tire pressure light is on, check the pressure before driving.

None of this is complicated. It's just habit-building. The drivers who skip this are the ones calling their parents from a parking lot because they didn't notice the flat tire until they heard the grinding.

---

### What Actually Happens

A 16-year-old backs out of a parking space and hits a shopping cart that was behind the car. He didn't walk around before getting in. The cart scratches a $3,000 panel. His parents' insurance goes up. He's embarrassed. All of this was preventable with a 10-second walk around the car.

Not a horror story — just the kind of thing that actually happens, constantly, because people skip the basics.

---

### Quick Recap

- [ ] Walk around the car before every drive
- [ ] Seat adjusted so you reach all pedals without stretching
- [ ] Mirrors set before you pull out — not while moving
- [ ] Phone away before you start
- [ ] Check for dashboard warning lights

**One thing to practice this week:** Do the full pre-drive checklist out loud, every time, until it feels automatic.

## MODULE 3 — DEFENSIVE DRIVING

### What It Actually Means

Defensive driving gets taught like it's about being overly cautious — hands at 10 and 2, never go over the speed limit, come to a complete stop for three full seconds. That's not what it is.

Real defensive driving is about reading what other drivers are going to do before they do it, and positioning yourself so that when someone does something dumb, you have an out.

You cannot control other drivers. You can only control your position, your speed, and your attention. That's it.

---

### The 3-Second Rule (And Why It's the Most Important Thing in This Book)

Pick an object on the side of the road. When the car in front of you passes it, count — one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand. You should reach that object no earlier than three seconds after the car in front of you did.

This is your following distance. This single habit prevents more accidents than anything else in this book.

Here's why: if the car in front of you hits its brakes hard right now, how long does it take you to:
1. See the brake lights
2. Decide to brake
3. Move your foot from gas to brake
4. Actually apply enough pressure to stop

That sequence takes about 1.5 seconds in ideal conditions, with a driver who's paying full attention. Then add the actual stopping distance for your speed. At 60 mph, you need at least 200 feet. The 3-second rule gives you roughly that buffer.

Most new drivers follow at 1 second. That's not a gap — that's a collision waiting for a reason.

---

### Reading Traffic Ahead

Don't just watch the car directly in front of you. Watch the car two or three cars ahead. If brake lights are appearing further up the road, you have extra seconds to ease off the gas — which means you brake later, slower, and smoother than the person who only watched the car in front of them.

This is called "scanning ahead" and experienced drivers do it constantly without thinking about it. You'll have to do it consciously until it becomes automatic.

**Practice:** On your next drive, try to keep at least two cars visible ahead of you whenever possible. Count how many times you see a situation developing before the car right in front of you reacts to it.

---

### The Out

Every time you're driving, you should have an answer to: "If the car in front of me stops right now, what do I do?"

On a normal road, the answer is usually "brake." On a highway at 70 mph with a car on each side and one behind, "brake" might not be enough. So you also think about: is there a shoulder? Is the right lane clear? Is there a gap I could steer into?

This sounds like a lot. It isn't — it becomes background processing within a few months. The point is to start thinking about it now, while it still feels conscious.

---

### Quick Recap

- [ ] Maintain 3-second following distance — always
- [ ] Scan ahead 2-3 cars, not just the car directly in front
- [ ] Always know your "out" — what you'd do if traffic stopped right now
- [ ] You can't control other drivers; control your position and attention

**One thing to practice this week:** Every time you're in the car, count your following distance. Adjust it until 3 seconds feels natural.

## MODULE 6 — THE STUFF THAT GETS TEENS KILLED

### Let's Be Direct About This

Teen drivers are involved in more fatal crashes per mile driven than any other age group. Not because teens are bad at driving mechanics. The mechanics come quickly. It's judgment — specifically, the failure to recognize when a situation is more dangerous than it feels.

This module isn't designed to scare you. It's designed to make sure you know exactly what the risks are, so you can make a real decision about them rather than an accidental one.

---

### Phones

You've heard this. You're going to hear it again.

At 60 mph, reading a text that takes 5 seconds means you traveled the length of a football field without looking at the road.

The reason people still do it isn't because they don't know it's dangerous. It's because they've done it 50 times without anything happening, and the brain learns: "this is probably fine." The brain is wrong. The 51st time might be the one where a kid runs into the road.

The solution isn't willpower. It's removal. Phone in the glovebox. Phone in your bag. Phone on Do Not Disturb. If you need GPS, mount it and set it before you start. If someone texts you and you're driving, they can wait 20 minutes.

If you're the kind of person who thinks "I can check it at a red light" — that still counts as distracted driving and is still illegal in most states. The light turns green faster than you expect.

---

### Passengers

Having one passenger your age in the car roughly doubles your crash risk. Two passengers roughly triples it.

This is well-documented and consistent across studies. It's not about the distraction (though that's part of it) — it's about the social pressure to seem relaxed, to show off slightly, to not be "that person" who drives slowly and carefully in front of their friends.

The fix: decide before you get in the car that you don't care what they think about your driving. You are the one with hands on the wheel. Their opinion of your speed has no value.

---

### Fatigue

Driving while severely tired is comparable to driving at the legal alcohol limit for adults. Your reaction time slows. You stop scanning ahead. You make small steering corrections that you don't notice.

Signs you're too tired to drive: you've been awake over 18 hours, you caught yourself zoning out in the last 10 minutes, or you literally cannot remember the last half mile you drove.

The right move is always to stop. Park, call someone, or take 20 minutes. The embarrassment of admitting you're too tired to drive is worth less than nothing compared to what happens if you don't.

---

### Quick Recap

- [ ] Phone away before you start — not in hand, not on the seat
- [ ] More passengers = more risk. Know this going in.
- [ ] If you're tired enough to notice it, you're too tired to drive
- [ ] These aren't rules someone made up. They're patterns from thousands of crashes.

**One thing to practice this week:** Drive one trip with your phone completely out of reach. Notice how often you feel the urge to check it.
